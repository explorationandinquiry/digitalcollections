Strategic Search

Module overview

UCLA Library’s digital collections include materials from different communities and histories, representing a rich resource for research. However, with thousands of digital images and assets available, finding the resources you need can feel overwhelming. This 4-part module will help you develop fundamental search strategies that will enable you to effectively navigate digital collections—plus, it will unearth some of the treasures of the digital collection.

Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Understand the difference between faceted search and keyword search, and when to use both
  • Generate a list of keywords for searches, including synonyms, related terms, broader terms, and narrower terms
  • Confidently use different aspects of a digital collections’ interface 
  • Use different search strategies to identify materials, including divergent (i.e. brainstorming) and convergent (i.e. selection) search

Part I: Keyword and Faceted Search

Digital collections have a couple of pathways into the items. Depending on where you are at in your research, some may be more helpful than others. You’ll likely use multiple search techniques throughout the course of your work.

Keyword search is perhaps the most familiar. Similar to Google and other search engines, a keyword search will retrieve items that have that keyword within their description (also known as “metadata”).

Sometimes, if the image is text-based, such as an image of a newspaper, the image will be processed with optical character recognition (OCR), so that a keyword search would also retrieve items that have the keyword within the text of the image as well. However, OCR is not always used by digital collections, and is often inaccurate. A keyword search thus will not always retrieve every item that has that keyword within the image itself (see the below image).

When should you use keyword search? Keyword search is great for when you have a specific topic in mind, and you think it is likely that images will have metadata that includes that keyword.

Faceted browse, on the other hand, involves filtering search results by category. You can filter search results by categories within the item’s description, or metadata, such as location, date, or language. See the below image for where the faceted browse is located on the UCLA Library Digital Collections.

When should you use faceted browse? Faceted browse is helpful for browsing materials, when you are at the beginning stages of your research. Faceted search can also establish helpful parameters for search results that could not be accomplished via keywords, such as limiting by date.

Keyword and faceted search are often used together, such as entering a certain keyword and then limiting results by a certain location.

Part II: Generating Keywords

An important part of keyword search is generating keywords. Research is an iterative process—meaning that the results of your initial search will often inform the results of your next search. In addition to the terms that immediately come to mind, think of terms related to your subject, or synonyms and alternate spellings for your keywords. This is especially important for names—a name like Robert may be cataloged as Robert, Rob, or Bob within an item’s metadata.

As for related terms, what concepts related to your subject of interest? What about broader or narrower ideas related to your keyword?

For example, let’s say you were researching minimum wage legislation within the United States. Possible keywords include:


  • INITIAL KEYWORD - minimum wage legislation
  • SYNONYMS - minimum wage law, minimum wage statute 
  • RELATED TERMS - Department of Labor, living wage, inflation indexing, cost of living
  • BROADER TERMS - labor laws, labor legislation
  • NARROWER TERMS - federal minimum wage, state minimum wage, citywide minimum wage

Another helpful search tool when using keyword search is the asterisk (*) wildcard operator, which retrieves alternatives and plurals of the keyword. Accordingly, a keyword search for Rob* will retrieve Rob, Robert, robes, Robinson, robot, etc.

Part III: Browsing by Subject

When librarians catalog items, they often assign a “subject”—a term that describes what the item is about. This can be a useful facet for browsing, and can also show research strengths of a digital collection. A look at the subject facet for UCLA Library, for example, shows that as of August 2020, the collection had many items concerning law, entertainment, and the environment (see below image).

In addition to this list of subjects for the entire collection, individual items have subjects, all of which were hyperlinked. This can be a helpful way to find a niche topic and items within the collection related to that topic.

An important thing to remember when browsing any library collection, especially by subject, is that all of this metadata is human-created. Human catalogers bring to their work all of their assumptions, worldviews, and biases. Browsing by subject can be a helpful way to source materials, but it is important to understand these descriptors as the work product of a library worker, and not absolute truth or always fully representative or inclusive.
